# Chapter 2: The Four Dimensions: A Matrix for Pattern Analysis

 

On a volcanic hillside above Lake Batur in central Bali, a priest performs calculations that will determine when twelve hundred farmer collectives flood their rice paddies. He consults astronomical tables inscribed on palm-leaf manuscripts, tracks the lunar calendar, and considers reports from downstream temple networks about water availability and pest pressures. The ritual he conducts appears purely spiritual—offerings of flowers and rice, prayers in Sanskrit, meditation before shrine statues of the rice goddess Dewi Sri. Yet this ceremony coordinates an irrigation system spanning hundreds of square kilometers, managing water distribution across a complex watershed without written contracts, government enforcement, or digital communication.

 

The Balinese water temple system presents a puzzle that cannot be solved by examining religion alone, or ecology alone, or social organization alone. The priests are not merely performing symbolic acts; they are executing sophisticated calculations. The farmers are not simply following tradition; they are solving collective action problems that modern agricultural science failed to improve upon. When Indonesia's government introduced Green Revolution technologies in 1971—high-yield rice varieties, chemical pesticides, instructions to "plant as often as possible"—rice yields paradoxically declined and pest damage increased. The system recovered only when traditional water temple authority was restored in the 1980s, after anthropologist Stephen Lansing demonstrated through complexity modeling that the priests had been optimizing outcomes the whole time.

 

How does a religious ritual regulate ecology at watershed scale? The answer requires examining four dimensions simultaneously: the cognitive architecture that enables priests to perform hydrological calculations through ritual practice, the social institutions that grant those calculations authority, the ecological constraints that make coordination necessary, and the historical trajectory that built these systems over centuries. Remove any single dimension and the pattern collapses. This chapter provides the analytical framework for examining such patterns—not as isolated phenomena but as multi-dimensional complexes where cognition, society, ecology, and history interact.

 

## 2.1 The Cognitive Dimension

 

Religious patterns operate through human minds, and human minds operate through specific cognitive mechanisms shaped by evolution. We do not think about the sacred with general-purpose reasoning applied equally to all domains. We deploy specialized mental architectures—what cognitive scientists call domain-specific modules—that evolved to solve particular ancestral problems: detecting agents with minds, avoiding contamination, forming coalitions, navigating social hierarchies. Religion does not bypass these systems. It exploits them.

 

The Balinese priest consulting astronomical tables engages multiple cognitive capacities simultaneously. His folk physics intuitions about water flow combine with folk biology knowledge of rice cultivation, layered with culturally transmitted understanding of ritual calendars. He detects patterns in celestial movements using the same spatial reasoning humans evolved for navigation. He represents the rice goddess Dewi Sri as an agent with intentions—a being who can be pleased or angered—activating what Justin Barrett calls the Hyperactive Agency Detection Device (HADD). This cognitive module scans the environment for minds, better to mistake wind in the grass for a predator than to miss an actual threat. Religious thought piggybacks on this system, populating the world with gods, spirits, ancestors—minimally counterintuitive agents that violate one or two expectations while conforming to most.

 

Pascal Boyer's work on "Religion Explained" demonstrates why some religious concepts spread while others fade. Ideas that are entirely intuitive prove forgettable—a rock that behaves like a normal rock tells no interesting story. Ideas that are excessively counterintuitive become incoherent—a rock that reads minds, speaks French, grants wishes, and travels backward in time exceeds cognitive processing capacity. But minimally counterintuitive concepts—a rock that hears prayers, or a river that possesses consciousness—stick in memory precisely because they violate expectations in limited, tractable ways. The Ganges as goddess, not mere water channel, becomes culturally transmissible because it activates both folk biology (rivers flow downstream, carry sediment) and folk psychology (Ganga has intentions, responds to devotion).

 

Yet cognition extends beyond individual brains. The water temple system functions through distributed cognition—knowledge spread across a network of practitioners who individually know their local conditions but collectively optimize regional outcomes. No single priest calculates the entire watershed's planting schedule; each temple in the hierarchy coordinates with its neighbors through ritual timing. The system resembles a computational network where nodes process local information and transmit signals through ceremonial sequences. When upstream temples complete certain festivals, downstream temples know to begin specific agricultural phases. Knowledge lives in the practice itself, not in any individual's explicit understanding of the whole.

 

This embodied dimension of cognition appears throughout religious systems. A Muslim performing wudu—ritual ablution before prayer—does not merely think about purity in the abstract. The physical act of washing hands, mouth, face, and feet activates contamination intuitions hardwired into human psychology. We evolved to avoid pathogens; religion extends this visceral aversion to moral and spiritual domains. The body's revulsion at spoiled food becomes the template for pollution beliefs that structure entire cosmologies. Douglas's "Purity and Danger" showed how purification rituals map physical categories onto social boundaries—what belongs where, who may touch what, which mixings violate order. The ritual acts are the thinking, not merely symbols of thoughts occurring elsewhere.

 

Balinese priests learn their calculations through years of embodied apprenticeship. They do not study hydrological engineering in classrooms; they participate in ceremonies, copy astronomical texts, walk irrigation channels, observe elder priests making decisions. The knowledge is kinesthetic—knowing through doing. When external experts tried to rationalize the system by replacing ritual schedules with agronomic formulas, the optimization failed because the formula extracted from the embodied practice could not capture the practice's full complexity. The priests were not performing inferior versions of scientific calculation; they were executing a different kind of knowledge, one that existed in ritual performance itself.

 

## 2.2 The Social Dimension

 

Cognition generates religious possibilities; social structures determine which possibilities persist. A brilliant theological innovation that fails to solve coordination problems, establish authority, or transmit across generations leaves no trace in the archaeological or ethnographic record. Religious patterns survive because they organize human action in ways that matter to communities.

 

The Balinese water temple hierarchy exemplifies how ritual creates coordination without central command. Temples form a nested network: small field temples (*sanggah cawkadan*) serve individual irrigation units, larger temples (*ulun suwi*) coordinate multiple subaks, and mother temples like Pura Ulun Danu Batur at the volcanic caldera source oversee regional integration. Farmers could, in principle, negotiate water distribution through secular contracts or government bureaus. Instead, they defer to ritual authority because temple calendars provide something contracts cannot—common knowledge that everyone else is following the same schedule.

 

Michael Chwe's analysis of "Rational Ritual" explains this mechanism. When devotees gather at Kumbh Mela, bathing together in the sacred confluence, they accomplish more than individual purification. Each pilgrim observes millions of others participating in the same act, creating shared knowledge that "we all believe this is sacred enough to travel thousands of kilometers, endure immense crowds, risk sanitation hazards." That shared knowledge—I know, you know, I know you know, you know I know—enables coordination at scales that private belief cannot achieve. The ritual is the message: participation signals commitment in ways that verbal declarations or written contracts can easily fake.

 

This social function shapes what religious knowledge looks like and how it transmits. Water temple priests inherit their positions through patrilineal succession, ensuring continuity across generations. The apprentice priest learns not just ritual procedures but the social authority to make those procedures consequential. When an elder priest announces the timing for temple festivals, farmers comply not because they have verified the astronomical calculations but because the social institution grants that priest's pronouncements legitimacy. Authority and knowledge co-constitute each other: knowledge without authority is opinion, authority without knowledge is tyranny. Religious traditions navigate this tension through initiation systems that test, certify, and socialize knowledge-bearers.

 

Van Gennep identified the structure shared across initiation rites worldwide: separation from ordinary life, liminality where normal rules suspend, and reincorporation with new status. A novice entering a monastic order withdraws from secular society, spends years in disciplined practice where hierarchies of the outside world no longer apply, then returns with sacred authority. The initiation creates insiders and outsiders—those who have undergone the transformation and those who have not. Esoteric knowledge systems, from ancient mystery cults to tantric Buddhism, use this boundary to maintain group identity and regulate access to power. When everyone knows a ritual's meaning, it confers no special status; secrecy transforms knowledge into social capital.

 

These authority structures vary across traditions. Charismatic leaders like prophets derive legitimacy from perceived direct divine communication, creating unstable but potentially revolutionary social forms. Traditional authority, invested in hereditary priesthoods or elder councils, provides stability at the cost of adaptability. Rational-legal authority, codified in written canons and institutional hierarchies, enables scaling to civilizational levels but risks bureaucratic ossification. Each pattern solves different coordination problems and generates different vulnerabilities.

 

The transmission mechanisms matter as much as the content transmitted. Textual religions—Judaism, Christianity, Islam—achieved stability through written canonization but faced new problems: who interprets scripture, how to handle textual contradictions, what role oral tradition plays when written word exists. Oral traditions must encode knowledge in forms that survive retelling—rhyme, rhythm, formulaic phrases, vivid imagery. The Vedic tradition maintained elaborate mnemonic systems to preserve Sanskrit texts for millennia without writing, a cognitive feat requiring social institutions to support professional reciters. When traditions encounter each other, the social dimension determines outcomes: conversion, syncretism, conflict, mutual indifference. Religious patterns persist not because ideas naturally replicate but because social structures create incentives, sanctions, and transmission pathways that make persistence more likely than dissolution.

 

## 2.3 The Ecological Dimension

 

Religious patterns do not float in abstract conceptual space. They operate in environments with rivers that flood or dry up, crops that succeed or fail, diseases that spread or recede. The ecological dimension constrains which patterns can emerge and determines what functions they serve beyond whatever cognitive appeal or social utility they provide.

 

The Balinese water temple system solves a collective action problem inherent in wet rice agriculture on mountainous volcanic terrain. Rice requires carefully timed flooding; too much water drowns the plants, too little stunts growth. Paddies arranged on steep terraces share water sources, so upstream farmers' decisions affect downstream yields. Synchronized planting across a region creates abundant food for rice pests—rats, insects, diseases—which then reproduce exponentially. Desynchronized planting denies pests stable food supply, limiting population growth. But desynchronization requires coordination: if some farmers plant early and others late, the early planters face pest damage from adjacent fallow fields.

 

No individual farmer can optimize this system alone. No pair of neighboring farmers can optimize it. The optimal strategy requires watershed-level coordination across hundreds of paddies and dozens of water sources, accounting for pest dynamics, water availability, and crop timing. Traditional Indonesian rice farmers lacked computers, differential equations, or systems ecology textbooks. They had water temples.

 

The ritual calendar encodes the solution. Temple festivals mark planting dates; ceremonial cycles determine flooding schedules. Upstream temples coordinate with downstream temples through ritual timing—when one temple completes its Odalan anniversary ceremony, affiliated temples know to begin certain agricultural phases. The system emerged through centuries of cultural evolution: practices that improved yields survived, practices that degraded productivity were abandoned or modified. The result is a decentralized optimization algorithm run on ritual hardware.

 

When Indonesia's government introduced Green Revolution technologies—new rice varieties, chemical fertilizers, pesticides—agronomists assumed traditional practices represented suboptimal tradition awaiting modernization. The Ministry of Agriculture instructed farmers to "plant as often as possible" to maximize annual yields. Farmers abandoned staggered planting for synchronized cycles. Pest populations exploded. Rice blast fungus and brown planthopper infestations devastated yields despite massive pesticide application. The ecological system that ritual management had maintained for a millennium collapsed within a decade.

 

Lansing's intervention came through complexity modeling. He built an agent-based computer simulation of the water temple system, programming virtual farmers to make planting decisions based on local water availability and pest pressure. When simulated farmers coordinated through temple rituals, the system converged on near-optimal yields within a decade of virtual time. When simulated farmers acted independently or synchronized planting, performance declined. The model demonstrated what the Green Revolution obscured: the priests were not performing inefficient tradition, they were executing sophisticated ecological management through ritual technology.

 

Recovery required restoring not just the practices but the authority structures. Farmers who had been taught to ignore temple calendars needed institutional permission to resume traditional cycles. Government agencies that had positioned themselves as superior to "backward" ritual needed to acknowledge their failure. By the late 1980s, water temple authority was rehabilitated; by the 1990s, rice productivity recovered to pre-Green Revolution levels without chemical inputs. UNESCO designated the system a World Heritage site in 2012, recognizing it as cultural patrimony worth preserving.

 

This case reveals the ecological dimension's necessity for understanding religious patterns. Any purely cognitive account—priests perform rituals because agency detection modules generate spirit beliefs—cannot explain why this particular ritual system persists while countless others fade. Any purely social explanation—rituals create group cohesion—cannot account for the specific content of water temple ceremonies. The ecological function does not exhaust the pattern's meaning, but it explains the pattern's persistence. Rituals that enhanced food security across generations became embedded in social institutions; those that failed to manage resources were outcompeted or abandoned.

 

Sacred landscapes worldwide encode similar ecological knowledge. Taboos against hunting pregnant animals preserve breeding populations. Restrictions on harvesting certain plants during specific seasons allow regeneration. Sacred groves function as biodiversity reserves, protected by religious prohibition rather than secular law. Roy Rappaport's classic study of Tsembaga Maring pig festivals in Papua New Guinea showed how ritual cycles regulate warfare frequency, pig herd size, and population pressure in ways that maintain ecological balance. The participants need not consciously understand the system's function; the ritual works because it embeds ecological constraints in sacred obligation.

 

## 2.4 The Historical Dimension

 

Patterns do not emerge fully formed from timeless principles. They have histories—contingent origins, path-dependent trajectories, critical junctures where small differences produced large divergences. The Balinese water temple system exists because of specific historical developments that could have unfolded otherwise.

 

Archaeological and epigraphic evidence traces the system's origins to the ninth century, when Hindu-Buddhist kingdoms established centralized control over rice agriculture. Inscriptions on stone and copper plates record land grants to temples, irrigation rights, and ceremonial obligations. The early system likely involved royal authority coordinating agriculture through religious institutions, a pattern common across Southeast Asian mandala states. But the kingdoms fragmented, colonialism disrupted traditional authority structures, and Dutch administration imposed rationalized bureaucracy. The water temples persisted through these transformations not because they were unchanging tradition but because they adapted: when royal courts collapsed, temple networks absorbed coordination functions; when colonial taxation replaced religious tribute, temples maintained ritual calendars that continued to time agriculture.

 

This path dependence—the way early choices constrain later possibilities—appears throughout religious history. Christianity became textually oriented in part because the Roman Empire's literate administrative culture provided infrastructure for scriptural canonization. Islam's development as both religion and legal system reflected the Arabian Peninsula's commercial networks requiring contract law and dispute resolution. Chinese religions integrated with imperial bureaucracy, creating celestial hierarchies mirroring earthly administration. In each case, early institutional forms shaped what later developments could become.

 

Critical junctures—moments when trajectories could have branched—reveal contingency. The Green Revolution represents such a juncture for Balinese water temples. Had Lansing's research not demonstrated the system's sophistication, had Indonesian technocrats remained committed to agronomic modernization, the ritual coordination might have permanently dissolved. Younger generations might have adopted secular agricultural practices, elder priests might have lost apprentices, temple funding might have dried up. The pattern's survival was not inevitable.

 

Nor is survival always desirable from all perspectives. The Ganges pilgrimage system demonstrates how historical development creates ecological contradictions. Ancient pilgrimage routes connected sacred sites along the river's length, creating a spiritual geography that persisted for millennia. Medieval temple construction and royal patronage expanded the system. British colonial rail networks made pilgrimage accessible to millions who previously lacked transportation. Post-independence Indian nationalism embraced pilgrimage as cultural identity marker. Contemporary transportation—buses, trains, highways—brings tens of millions annually to bathe at Varanasi and other tirthas.

 

Each historical layer added to the system without removing prior elements. The result is mounting ecological crisis: untreated sewage from expanding cities, industrial effluent from factories, cremation ash from increasing funeral volume, agricultural runoff from Green Revolution fertilizers. The river sacred for its purity has become one of the world's most polluted water bodies. Pilgrims bathing for spiritual purification immerse themselves in water testing positive for fecal coliform bacteria thousands of times above safe levels. The historical dimension reveals why this pattern persists despite its contradictions—the accumulated weight of textual sacredness, institutional investment, nationalist symbolism, and personal devotion resists rational reformation.

 

Traditions change through both innovation and transmission. Buddhism began as reform movement within Hindu-majority context, innovated doctrines of non-self and dependent origination, spread across Asia through merchant networks and royal patronage, splintered into Theravada and Mahayana branches, absorbed local deities and practices through syncretism. Each phase involved historical contingencies: which monks traveled which trade routes, which kings converted and why, how textual canons were translated and interpreted. The pattern we recognize as Buddhism is a historical accumulation, not a pure essence unfolding according to internal logic.

 

Understanding this historical dimension prevents viewing patterns as either eternal universals or arbitrary constructions. Water temple coordination is neither timeless Balinese essence nor colonial invention. It emerged through specific historical processes, adapted to changing conditions, and persists because historical investments make abandonment costly. Recognizing path dependence clarifies why patterns cannot be easily transferred between contexts—transplanting water temple ritual to Nebraska irrigation districts would fail because the historical trajectory that made the ritual authoritative in Bali does not exist in Nebraska.

 

## 2.5 How Dimensions Interact: The Matrix Approach

 

Religious patterns operate simultaneously across cognitive, social, ecological, and historical dimensions. The methodological contribution of dimensional analysis lies not in identifying these aspects independently—scholars have long recognized that religion involves minds, communities, environments, and traditions—but in examining how dimensions interact to create emergent properties unpredictable from any single perspective.

 

Return to the Balinese water temple system with this integrative view. The cognitive dimension explains why priests can perform complex calculations through ritual practice: embodied knowledge, distributed cognition, specialized mental modules for spatial reasoning and pattern recognition. The social dimension explains why farmers defer to priestly authority: common knowledge formation through ritual participation, initiation systems that certify legitimate knowledge-bearers, hierarchical temple networks that coordinate local decisions. The ecological dimension explains what problems the system solves: watershed-scale pest management, irrigation coordination, sustainable rice yields. The historical dimension explains why this particular solution exists: ninth-century institutional origins, adaptation through colonial disruption, recovery after Green Revolution crisis.

 

Each dimension is necessary; none is sufficient. A cognitive account alone cannot explain why this ritual persists while equally cognitively appealing alternatives fade. A social account cannot explain the specific content of temple ceremonies or their timing. An ecological account cannot explain why ritual rather than secular contracts coordinates behavior. A historical account cannot explain how the system functions in the present. The pattern exists in the interaction.

 

Remove any single dimension and the system fails. Destroy the cognitive transmission—prevent priests from learning astronomical calculations and ritual procedures—and the coordination collapses even if social institutions and ecological needs remain. Eliminate the social authority—declare temple calendars legally meaningless—and farmers revert to independent decision-making despite cognitive capacity and ecological benefits. Alter the ecological context—shift to industrial agriculture that eliminates irrigation needs—and the rituals persist only as heritage performance, disconnected from environmental function. Erase the historical trajectory—through conquest, colonization, or catastrophic disruption—and reconstruction must start from scratch, lacking the accumulated institutional knowledge.

 

The matrix approach prevents reductionism in any direction. Cognitive reductionists sometimes claim that religious patterns can be fully explained by evolved mental mechanisms: humans believe in gods because HADD detects agents. But HADD generates promiscuous agency attributions—wind in grass, faces in clouds, conspiracies in random events—that do not crystallize into stable religious institutions unless social mechanisms select and stabilize certain attributions while discarding others. Ecological determinists claim religion reflects environmental constraints: desert peoples develop different cosmologies than rainforest peoples. True, but ecology does not determine which cosmologies emerge or why particular symbolic systems spread. Social constructivists argue religion is whatever communities make it: sacred categories are arbitrary conventions. Yet conventions must work within cognitive and ecological constraints; not any arbitrary system can persist across generations.

 

The Kumbh Mela illustrates how dimensional interaction scales to massive coordination challenges. Cognitively, the pilgrimage follows astronomical logic—planetary positions determined by Hindu cosmology indicate auspicious bathing times. Pilgrims engage embodied practice, immersing in sacred waters to purify karma, enacting beliefs through physical participation. Socially, the gathering creates common knowledge at unprecedented scale: when a hundred million people congregate, the event itself signals commitment that individual belief statements cannot convey. Government logistics, volunteer networks, monastic orders, ritual specialists coordinate to manage crowds. Ecologically, the concentration tests river capacity, sanitation infrastructure, disease transmission dynamics. The sacred belief that Ganges water cannot be polluted contradicts empirical evidence of contamination, creating unresolved tension. Historically, mythological origins (gods churning the cosmic ocean, nectar dropping at four sacred sites) provide narrative foundation, while modern transportation, telecommunications, and state infrastructure enable scales impossible in premodern contexts.

 

Dimensions can conflict rather than harmonize. Ganges pilgrimage is cognitively compelling (ritual purity), socially powerful (mass coordination, nationalist symbolism), and historically rooted (millennia-old texts). It is also ecologically catastrophic. No simple optimization reconciles these dimensions. Religious systems must navigate trade-offs, accepting suboptimal outcomes in some dimensions to preserve functions in others. The matrix approach makes these trade-offs visible rather than pretending all dimensions naturally align.

 

This framework does not claim that all religious patterns optimize all dimensions simultaneously. It claims that understanding any pattern requires examining all four, recognizing both their integration and their tensions. Some patterns persist despite ecological dysfunction because social and cognitive benefits outweigh environmental costs. Others fade despite cognitive appeal because they fail to solve coordination problems or adapt to changing contexts. The analyst's task is to identify which dimensions drive which outcomes and where conflicts create pressure for change.

 

## Pattern Detection Lab 2: Four-Dimensional Analysis

 

Apply the matrix framework to a ritual you know well—a wedding, funeral, holiday meal, or seasonal celebration. Work through each dimension systematically:

 

**Cognitive**: What mental operations does the ritual engage? Does it activate contamination intuitions (purity/pollution), agency detection (speaking to or about supernatural beings), social cognition (kinship categories, hierarchical relationships)? Is knowledge embodied in physical actions rather than verbal explanation? Where is cognition distributed across participants rather than concentrated in individual minds?

 

**Social**: What coordination problems does the ritual solve? Who has authority to perform or modify the ceremony? How do participants signal commitment or belonging? What boundaries does the ritual create between insiders and outsiders? How is ritual knowledge transmitted across generations—through apprenticeship, written instruction, or public participation?

 

**Ecological**: Does the ritual respond to environmental rhythms—seasonal agriculture, weather patterns, resource availability? Does it regulate behavior in ways that affect material outcomes—food distribution, labor organization, resource conservation? Are sacred objects or spaces tied to specific landscapes or ecosystems?

 

**Historical**: When did this ritual take form? What earlier practices did it build upon or react against? Have major disruptions—migration, colonization, technological change—altered its performance? What institutional investments make the ritual costly to abandon? Which elements resist change and which adapt readily?

 

Map the interactions. Where do dimensions reinforce each other—cognitive appeal supporting social coordination, ecological function stabilizing historical transmission? Where do they conflict—traditional practices clashing with modern environments, social hierarchies contradicting egalitarian cosmologies? Your analysis should reveal the ritual as multidimensional pattern rather than simple custom.

 

This exercise demonstrates that sophisticated pattern analysis requires holding multiple perspectives simultaneously. The temptation is to privilege one dimension—explaining everything through evolutionary psychology, or economic function, or social construction. Resist. The pattern exists in the interaction, and the interaction is where understanding lives.

## Chapter Summary (for continuity tracking)

 

**Core Argument**: Religious patterns operate simultaneously across four dimensions—cognitive (evolved mental mechanisms, embodied practice, distributed knowledge), social (authority structures, transmission systems, boundary formation), ecological (environmental constraints, resource management, landscape interaction), and historical (path dependence, contingency, innovation dynamics)—which interact to create emergent properties not predictable from any single perspective.

 

**Key Concepts Introduced**:

- Cognitive dimension: domain-specific modules, hyperactive agency detection, minimally counterintuitive concepts, embodied and distributed cognition

- Social dimension: common knowledge formation through ritual, initiation systems, authority structures (charismatic/traditional/rational-legal), transmission mechanisms

- Ecological dimension: collective action problems, ritual as resource management technology, environmental constraints on pattern persistence

- Historical dimension: path dependence, critical junctures, contingency in pattern origins and trajectories

- Matrix approach: dimensional interaction as analytical method, avoiding reductionism while recognizing tensions and trade-offs

 

**Major Examples Used**:

- Balinese water temple system (primary case study): nine-century-old ritual network coordinating watershed-scale irrigation and pest management, disrupted by Green Revolution and recovered through recognition of sophisticated ecological optimization

- Kumbh Mela: massive Hindu pilgrimage demonstrating cognitive (astronomical/mythological logic), social (common knowledge at scale), ecological (infrastructure stress), and historical (textual origins, modern transportation) dimensions

- Ganges sacred geography: pilgrimage system showing dimensional conflict between spiritual purity beliefs and ecological pollution

- Initiation rites: cross-cultural pattern of knowledge transmission through embodied practice and social boundary formation

The Balinese case demonstrated that patterns exist in dimensional interaction; Chapter 3 shows how to detect and map such patterns systematically across disparate traditions.
